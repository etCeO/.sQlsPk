📖 How Pig Latin Works

Master the rules of this playful language through interactive examples.

1
Vowel-first words
Words starting with a vowel (A, E, I, O, U) simply get "yay" added to the end. Easy!
apple → appleyay  |  over → overyay
2
Single consonant words
Move the first consonant to the end of the word, then add "ay".
pig → igpay  |  dog → ogday
3
Consonant cluster words
Move the entire consonant cluster (multiple consonants at the start) to the end, then add "ay".
string → ingstray  |  flip → ipflay
4
Capitalization preserved
The first letter of the Pig Latin word gets the same capitalization as the original word's first letter.
Hello → Ellohay  |  WORLD → ORLDWAY (first letter only)
5
Punctuation stays put
Leading and trailing punctuation is stripped before translation and reattached afterward, keeping your sentence structure intact.
"Hello!" → "Ellohay!"
6
Yay variant
Some versions use "way" instead of "yay" for vowel-starting words. Both are valid dialects of Pig Latin!
apple → appleway (variant)

🌍 Language Fun Facts

Trivia about Pig Latin and language play.

🎙 Ancient origins
Pig Latin is a form of language game called an "argot." References date back to the 1800s, though the modern "ay" version became popular in the 20th century.
🌐 Not just English
Many cultures have their own versions of language games — Spanish has "Jerigonza," French has "Verlan" (syllable reversal), and Swedes have "Rövarspråket."
🧠 Linguistic cousin
Language games like Pig Latin are studied in linguistics as examples of morphological play — manipulation of a language's smallest meaningful units (morphemes).
📺 Pop culture
Pig Latin appears in films, TV shows, and literature as a secret language between characters. It also appears in the famous toast "Oday ouyay eakspay Igpay Atinlay?"
